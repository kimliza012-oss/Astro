First Mars Crew Completes Year -
Long Simulated
Red Planet Mission

The inaugural CHAPEA (Crew Health and Performance Exploration Analog) crew—Kelly Haston (commander), Ross Brockwell, Nathan Jones, and Anca Selariu—completed a 378 - day simulated Mars mission, exiting the Mars Dune Alpha habitat on July 6, 2024.
The CHAPEA mission forms a vital bridge in NASA's Moon-to-Mars strategy, complementing Artemis lunar missions. Together, these analogs and real missions will provide the layered experience needed for sustainable and safe Mars exploration.

Andromeda Galaxy

The Andromeda Galaxy, also known as M31, is the closest spiral galaxy to our own Milky Way and sits approximately 2.5 million light-years from Earth. It's one of the few galaxies visible to the naked eye and appears as a faint, elongated blur in the night sky under dark conditions. Despite its distance, it's incredibly massive — containing around one trillion stars, more than double the estimated number in the Milky Way. Andromeda is moving toward us at an astonishing speed of 110 kilometers per second, and in about 4 billion years, it will collide with the Milky Way in a dramatic cosmic event. This future merger will not result in a catastrophic explosion but rather a graceful blending of both galaxies, likely forming a large elliptical galaxy often nicknamed “Milkomeda.” Interestingly, the galaxy gets its name from the Greek mythological princess Andromeda and lies within the constellation that also bears her name. With its majestic spiral arms and fascinating destiny, Andromeda offers a glimpse not just into the universe's structure but also into our galaxy's future.

Birth of a Star

Stars are born deep within vast, cold clouds of gas and dust in space, known as nebulae. These clouds, made primarily of hydrogen and helium, are scattered throughout galaxies and often appear as dark, quiet regions in the night sky. The process of star formation begins when a disturbance—such as the shockwave from a nearby supernova explosion or the collision of galaxies—causes a portion of the nebula to become gravitationally unstable. This region starts to collapse inward under its own gravity, and as it contracts, the mate rial within it clumps together and heats up, forming a dense, hot core called a protostar. At this stage, the protostar is not yet a full-fledged star; it glows faintly due to the heat from gravitational compression, not from nuclear fusion. Over time, the protostar continues to gather mass from its surroundings, and the temperature and pressure in i ts core steadily rise. When the core temperature reaches about 10 million degrees Cel sius, hydrogen atoms begin to fuse into helium in a process called nuclear fusion. Th is marks the true birth of a star. The energy released by fusion creates an outward p ressure that balances the inward pull of gravity, allowing the star to reach a stable state. This balance is called hydrostatic equilibrium, and the star enters what’s known as the main sequence phase of its life. In this phase, the star shines steadily for millions to billions of years, depending on its mass, fueled by the continuous fusion o f hydrogen into helium at its core.
